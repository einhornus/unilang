Etymology

From Middle English">Middle English hummen (“to hum, buzz, drone, make a murmuring sound to cover embarrassment”); akin to Dutch hommelen (“to bumble, buzz”), dialectal Dutch hommen (“to buzz, hum”), Middle High German">Middle High German hummen (“to hum”), probably ultimately of imitative origin.

Noun

hum (plural hums)

  1. A hummed tune, i.e. created orally with lips closed.
  2. An often indistinct sound resembling human humming.
    They could hear a hum coming from the kitchen, and found the dishwasher on.
    • circa">c. 1606, William Shakespeare, “The Tragedie of Macbeth”, in <cite>Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies: Published According to the True Originall Copies (First Folio">First Folio), London: Printed by Isaac Iaggard, and Ed[ward] Blount, published 1623, OCLC external text" href="http://worldcat.org/oclc/606515358">606515358, [Act III, scene ii]:
      the shard-borne beetle with his drowsy hums
  3. Busy activity, like the buzz of a beehive.
  4. (Great Britain">Britain, slang) unpleasant odour.
  5. (dated) An imposition or hoax; humbug.
  6. (obsolete) A kind of strong drink.
    (Can we find and add a quotation of Beaumont and Fletcher to this entry?)
  7. A phenomenon, or collection of phenomena, involving widespread reports of a persistent and invasive low-frequency humming, rumbling, or droning noise not audible to all people.
