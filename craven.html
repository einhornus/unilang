craven (19526)


Etymology

From Middle English craven (adjective).

Pronunciation

  • IPA(key): /ˈkɹeɪ.vən/
    • Audio (UK)
      		(file)
  • Rhymes: -eɪvən

Adjective

craven (comparative more craven, superlative most craven)

  1. Unwilling to fight; lacking even the rudiments of courage; extremely cowardly.
    • 1808 February 22, Walter Scott, “(please specify the introduction or canto number, or chapter name)”, in <cite>Marmion; a Tale of Flodden Field, Edinburgh: Printed by J[ames] Ballantyne and Co. for Archibald Constable and Company, […]; London: William Miller, and John Murray, <small>OCLC 270129616:
      The poor craven bridegroom said never a word.

Noun

craven (plural cravens)

  1. A coward.
    • 1599, William Shakespeare, “The Life of Henry the Fift”, in <cite>Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies: Published According to the True Originall Copies (First Folio), London: Printed by Isaac Iaggard, and Ed[ward] Blount, published 1623, <small>OCLC 606515358, [Act IV, scene vii]:
      He is a craven and a villain else.

Verb

craven (third-person singular simple present cravens, present participle cravening, simple past and past participle cravened)

  1. To make craven.
    • 1609: William Shakespeare, The Tragedy of Cymbeline, Act III, Scene IV
      There is a prohibition so divine / That cravens my weak hand.
