heart (610)


Etymology

From Middle English herte, from Old English heorte (“heart”), from Proto-Germanic *hertô (“heart”), from Proto-Indo-European *ḱḗr (“heart”). Cognate with Saterland Frisian Haat, West Frisian hert, Dutch hart, German Low German Hart, German Herz, Icelandic hjarta, Latin cor, cordis, Ancient Greek καρδία (kardía), Sanskrit हृद् (hṛd), Welsh craidd, Irish croí, Armenian սիրտ (sirt), Russian се́рдце (sérdce), and Lithuanian širdis. Doublet of cardia.

Most of the modern figurative senses (such as passion or compassion, spirit, inmost feelings, especially love, affection, and courage) were present in Old English. However, the meaning "inner part of something" dates from the early 14th century.[1]

The verb sense “to love” is from the 1977 I ❤ NY advertising campaign.[1]
