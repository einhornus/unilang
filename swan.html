Etymology 1

From Middle English">Middle English swan, from Old English ang">swan, from Proto-Germanic *swanaz (“swan”, literally “the singing bird”), from Proto-Indo-European *swonh₂-/*swenh₂- (“to sing, make sound”). Related also to Old English ang">ġeswin (“melody, song”), Old English ang">swinsian (“to make melody”), Latin sonus (“sound”), Russian звон (zvon, “ringing”), Russian звук (zvuk, “sound”).

Noun

swan (plural swans or swan)

  1. Any of various species of large, long-necked waterfowl, of genus Cygnus (bird family: Anatidae), most of which have white plumage.
  2. (figuratively) One whose grace etc. suggests a swan.
  3. (heraldry) This bird used as a heraldic charge, sometimes with a crown around its neck (e. g. the arms of Buckinghamshire).
